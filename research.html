Publication & Preprint

  • VaCDA: Variational Contrastive Alignment-based Scalable Human Activity Recognition
    Authors: Soham Khisa, Avijoy Chakma
    Conference: International Conference on Machine Learning and Applications (ICMLA 2025)
    [PAPER]
    Technological advancements have led to the rise of wearable devices with sensors that continuously monitor user activities, generating vast amounts of unlabeled data. This data is challenging to interpret, and manual annotation is labor-intensive and error-prone. Additionally, data distribution is often heterogeneous due to device placement, type, and user behavior variations. As a result, traditional transfer learning methods perform suboptimally, making it difficult to recognize daily activities. To address these challenges, we use a variational autoencoder (VAE) to learn a shared, low-dimensional latent space from available sensor data. This space generalizes data across diverse sensors, mitigating heterogeneity and aiding robust adaptation to the target domain. We integrate contrastive learning to enhance feature representation by aligning instances of the same class across domains while separating different classes. We propose Variational Contrastive Domain Adaptation (VaCDA), a multi-source domain adaptation framework combining VAEs and contrastive learning to improve feature representation and reduce heterogeneity between source and target domains. We evaluate VaCDA on multiple publicly available datasets across three heterogeneity scenarios: cross-person, cross-position, and cross-device. VaCDA outperforms the baselines in cross-position and cross-device scenarios.

  • Re-educating Educated Ones: A Case Study on Chakma Language Revitalization in Chittagong Hill Tracts
    Authors: Avijoy Chakma, Adity Khisa, Soham Khisa, Jannatun Noor, Sharifa Sultana
    Conference: Conference on Human Factors in Computing Systems (CHI 2026)
    [PAPER]
    Indigenous languages face significant cultural oppression from official state languages, particularly in the Global South. We investigate the Bangladeshi Chakma language revitalization movement, a community grappling with language liquidity and amalgamation into the dominant Bengali language. Our six-month-long qualitative study involving interviews and focus group discussions with Chakma language learning stakeholders uncovered existing community socio-economic challenges and resilience strategies. We noted the need for culturally grounded digital tools and resources. We propose an ICT-mediated community-centric framework for Indigenous language revitalization in the Global South, emphasizing the integration of historical identity elements, stakeholder-defined requirements, and effective digital engagement strategies to empower communities in preserving their linguistic and cultural heritage.

  • ChakmaNMT: Machine Translation for a Low-Resource and Endangered Language via Transliteration
    Authors: Aunabil Chakma, Aditya Chakma, Soham Khisa, Chumui Tripura, Masum Hasan, Rifat Shahriyar
    Workshop: EMNLP 2024 @ Workshop on Asian Translations
    [PAPER]
    We present the first systematic study of machine translation for Chakma, an endangered and extremely low-resource Indo-Aryan language, with the goal of supporting language access and preservation. We introduce a new Chakma-Bangla parallel and monolingual dataset, along with a trilingual Chakma-Bangla-English benchmark for evaluation. To address script mismatch and data scarcity, we propose a character-level transliteration framework that exploits the close orthographic and phonological relationship between Chakma and Bangla, preserving semantic content while enabling effective transfer from Bangla and multilingual pretrained models. We benchmark from-scratch MT, fine-tuned pretrained models, and large language models via in-context learning. Results show that transliteration is essential and that fine-tuning and in-context learning substantially outperform from-scratch baselines, with strong asymmetry across translation directions.

Ongoing Work

  • A New Approach for Improving Performance in the Novel Class Discovery Problem

    The novel class discovery problem in computer vision involves identifying and classifying previously unseen object categories without prior labeled examples. To address this challenge, we are developing a new approach aimed at enhancing overall performance and improving the model’s ability to generalize to new categories effectively.

  • Investigating Privacy Implications of LLMs in Smart Home Environments

    Large Language Models (LLM) integrated into smart home systems raise many concerns pertaining to privacy, concerns related to data collection, user interaction, and breaches regarding sensitive information. The work investigates the privacy implications of Large Language Model deployment within smart home environments in an attempt to outline vulnerabilities and suggest solutions for risk mitigation while preserving user confidentiality and trust.

Undergraduate Thesis

  • Knowledge Graph-Based Categorization of Newspaper Articles in a Newspaper Corpus

    Supervisor: Dr. Muhammad Masroor Ali
    Authors: Soham Khisa, Istiak Bin Mahmud
    Newspaper annotation involves enriching articles with structured, meaningful information to improve the accuracy of information retrieval, which is vital given the vast number of online news sources. While search engines often return imprecise results, semantic search offers a more refined approach by using knowledge graphs to better understand and categorize information. Our thesis introduces a system that categorizes news articles by extracting metadata through ontology, using a widely recognized taxonomy to classify the content.
